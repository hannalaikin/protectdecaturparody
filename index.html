WHAT CAN I DO?

A field guide for the performative citizen who wants to be involved, sound very concerned, and avoid reading anything longer than a headline.

  1. 1. Attend every public meeting, prepared to ignore facts.

    Bring pre printed talking points. Do not read staff reports. Do not listen. Interrupt politely at first, then loudly.

  2. 2. Demand community input, then reject all outcomes.

    Participate enthusiastically unless the decision does not go your way. In that case, claim the process was rigged.

  3. 3. Chant slogans instead of asking questions.

    Complex topics like zoning, utilities, or environmental controls are best addressed with yelling.

  4. 4. Coordinate applause, boos, and synchronized outrage.

    Bonus points if you clap mid sentence while someone else is speaking.

  5. 5. Hold hands dramatically.

    It does not change the vote, but it photographs well.

  6. 6. Recruit friends from outside the community.

    Local issue. No problem. Bring reinforcements who have never been here but feel very strongly anyway.

  7. 7. Label disagreement as hate.

    If someone supports the project, do not debate them. Assign a motive.

  8. 8. Repeat claims even after they have been corrected.

    Consistency matters more than accuracy.

  9. 9. Accuse experts of lying, then demand expert studies.

    Disregard the studies once they arrive.

  10. 10. If all else fails, shout follow the money.

    No need to specify whose or how. Just gesture vaguely.

Important note

Actual civic engagement usually works better with listening, facts, and good faith discussion. It just is not nearly as theatrical.

Why are residents fighting back?

Because progress is inconvenient.

Higher electricity and water bills, maybe someday

We prefer an aging grid held together with duct tape. Modern infrastructure threatens the comfort of rolling blackouts, surprise outages, and infrastructure failures we have grown used to.

Property values might go up, and that is a problem

Rising property values could increase equity, attract reinvestment, and improve public services. Naturally, we oppose all of that. Decline is predictable. Growth is scary.

Health risks, theoretical, speculative, selectively framed

Although backup generators run rarely and under strict regulations, we prefer to imagine them operating all day in a permanent cloud of doom. Actual nearby highways, warehouses, delivery trucks, and existing industrial uses are familiar and therefore harmless.

Noise concerns

Quiet enclosed buildings with limited traffic are unacceptable. We strongly favor constant diesel truck traffic, backup beeping, and overnight loading docks. Those noises feel more authentic.

Light pollution

Security lighting that meets code and shuts down when not needed is deeply offensive. We prefer the warm glow of underfunded streetlights, broken fixtures, and dark corridors that discourage walking and safety.

Our real concern, just being honest

  • We oppose high paying jobs.
  • We oppose lower traffic than warehouses.
  • We oppose new tax revenue for schools, police, and fire.
  • We oppose planning for the AI driven economy.
  • We oppose anything new, especially if it works.

In summary

We are not anti data center. We are pro decline, pro status quo, and pro complaint. And we will fight progress quietly, loudly, and on the internet.

Upcoming meetings and opportunities for outrage

Attendance encouraged. Listening discouraged.

Public hearing

Arrive early. Leave before answers are given. Be as disruptive and loud and unfriendly as possible.

Shout down the presenters. Try to get removed for television.

Community forum

Ask questions already answered online. Ignore any materials that were mailed, posted, or explained three times.

Repeat your question louder if you do not like the answer.

Vote night

Claim the process was unfair regardless of outcome.

If you win, say it was because the people spoke. If you lose, say it was corruption.
