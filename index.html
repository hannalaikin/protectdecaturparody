What?

Sabey Data Centers is proposing an approximately 900,000 square foot data center campus in Decatur Township, designed with two primary buildings, secure infrastructure, and closed loop cooling that uses a one time initial water fill and then office scale annual water use.

Where?

The campus is planned between Kentucky Avenue and Camby Road in Decatur Township. Site map and rendering from the project materials can be placed here by the site owner to show how the buildings sit on the land.

About the project

The land is already zoned for intense distribution uses. A previously approved plan allowed six large warehouse buildings, heavy truck traffic, and visible loading activity. Sabey’s proposal replaces that with two secure data center buildings, fewer peak hour trips, and enclosed equipment that is designed to operate quietly with significant landscaped buffers.

Cooling for the computers uses a closed loop system that is designed to minimize water use. The system needs a one time initial load of water and then uses annual volumes that are in the range of a typical office building for restrooms, kitchen spaces, landscaping, and humidity control. This is a different profile from traditional industrial facilities that rely on constant once through water use.

Power for the campus is provided through the regulated utility process, where any major investment is reviewed in public rate cases and long term resource plans. Data center customers pay for customer specific infrastructure and dedicated facilities, and in many cases large steady loads can help spread fixed costs over more units of electricity sold. That is how utilities often describe large users that can help stabilize the grid and support long term upgrades.

During construction, the project supports hundreds of union trades jobs. In operation, the campus is expected to support about one hundred permanent, skilled roles on site along with indirect work for local businesses that serve the facility and its employees. Property and equipment taxes contribute to schools, public safety, and local services over the life of the project.

Reasonable people can still disagree about land use, priorities, and what kind of growth they want to see. This page focuses on how opposition can sometimes slide from informed debate into performance, even when the underlying facts about water, traffic, power, and jobs are available in public documents.

Why are residents “fighting back”?

Because progress is inconvenient.

Higher electricity and water bills, maybe someday

We prefer an aging grid held together with duct tape. Modern infrastructure threatens the comfort of rolling blackouts, surprise outages, and infrastructure failures we have grown used to.

Property values might go up, and that is a problem

Rising property values could increase equity, attract reinvestment, and improve public services. Naturally, we oppose all of that. Decline is predictable. Growth is scary.

Health risks

Although backup generators run rarely and under strict regulations, we prefer to imagine them operating all day in a permanent cloud of doom. Actual nearby highways, warehouses, delivery trucks, and existing industrial uses are familiar and therefore harmless.

Noise concerns

Quiet enclosed buildings with limited traffic are unacceptable. We strongly favor constant diesel truck traffic, backup beeping, and overnight loading docks. Those noises feel more authentic.

Light pollution

Security lighting that meets code and shuts down when it is not needed is deeply offensive. We prefer the warm glow of underfunded streetlights, broken fixtures, and dark corridors that discourage walking and safety.

Our real concern, just being honest

  • We oppose high paying jobs.
  • We oppose lower traffic than warehouses.
  • We oppose new tax revenue for schools, police, and fire.
  • We oppose planning for the AI driven economy.
  • We oppose anything new, especially if it works.

In summary

We are not anti data center. We are pro decline, pro status quo, and pro complaint. And we will fight progress quietly, loudly, and on the internet.

What can I do?

A field guide for the performative citizen who wants to be involved, sound very concerned, and avoid reading anything longer than a headline.

  1. 1. Attend every public meeting, prepared to ignore facts.

    Bring pre printed talking points. Do not read staff reports. Do not listen. Interrupt politely at first, then loudly.

  2. 2. Demand community input, then reject all outcomes.

    Participate enthusiastically unless the decision does not go your way. In that case, claim the process was rigged.

  3. 3. Chant slogans instead of asking questions.

    Complex topics like zoning, utilities, or environmental controls are best addressed with yelling.

  4. 4. Coordinate applause, boos, and synchronized outrage.

    Bonus points if you clap mid sentence while someone else is speaking.

  5. 5. Hold hands dramatically.

    It does not change the vote, but it photographs well.

  6. 6. Recruit friends from outside the community.

    Local issue. No problem. Bring reinforcements who have never been here but feel very strongly anyway.

  7. 7. Label disagreement as hate.

    If someone supports the project, do not debate them. Assign a motive.

  8. 8. Repeat claims even after they have been corrected.

    Consistency matters more than accuracy.

  9. 9. Accuse experts of lying, then demand expert studies.

    Disregard the studies once they arrive.

  10. 10. If all else fails, shout follow the money.

    No need to specify whose or how. Just gesture vaguely.

Important note

Actual civic engagement usually works better with listening, facts, and good faith discussion. It just is not nearly as theatrical.

What else can I do?

Choose your favorite expression of unresearched passion. Every action here is a parody action.

Donate to the cause

Help fund more clipboards, matching T shirts, and outrage graphics. All donations go toward vibes, slogans, and printing the same flyer again.

Request a yard sign

Let your neighbors know where you stand without needing to explain why.

  • NO, do not ask me for details
  • SAVE OUR COMMUNITY
  • I READ THE HEADLINE

Email the city planner

We have drafted a template so you do not have to understand the project. Just copy, paste, and send. No reading required.

“I am deeply concerned and demand action immediately.”

Concern subject to change based on Facebook comments. Bonus points for copying people who are not involved.

Sign the petition

Join thousands of people who agree something feels wrong even if we cannot say what.

Facts optional. Signature permanent.

Signing does not require residency.

Calendar of meetings

Upcoming meetings and opportunities to be outraged. Attendance encouraged. Listening discouraged.

Public hearing

Arrive early. Leave before answers are given. Be as disruptive and loud and unfriendly as possible.

Shout down the presenters. Try to get removed for television.

Community forum

Ask questions already answered online. Ignore any materials that were mailed or posted.

Repeat your question louder if you do not like the answer.

Vote night

Claim the process was unfair regardless of outcome.

If you win, say it was because the people spoke. If you lose, say it was corruption.
