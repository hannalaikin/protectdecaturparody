Protecting Our Community Against 900,000 sq. ft. AI Data Center

A straight faced look at how big numbers and future scenarios are used when a new building appears on the map.

What

Sabey Corporation is a data center developer planning a campus of about nine hundred thousand square feet with power service of two hundred fifty megawatts. The figures sound dramatic in a sentence which is why they are repeated often.

Where

The campus is proposed between Kentucky Avenue and Camby Road in Decatur Township. For many people the space has lived as open land and flexible imagination. Any specific project will feel like too much for someone.

Why are residents “fighting back”?

Because progress is inconvenient.

Higher electricity & water bills (maybe, someday)

We prefer an aging grid held together with duct tape. Modern infrastructure threatens the comfort of rolling blackouts, surprise outages, and infrastructure failures we’ve grown used to.

Property values might go up — and that’s a problem

Rising property values could:

  • Increase equity
  • Attract reinvestment
  • Improve public services

Naturally, we oppose all of that. Decline is predictable. Growth is scary.

Health risks

Although backup generators run rarely and under strict regulations, we prefer to imagine them operating 24/7 in a permanent cloud of doom. Actual nearby highways, warehouses, delivery trucks, and existing industrial uses are familiar — and therefore harmless.

Noise concerns

Quiet, enclosed buildings with limited traffic are unacceptable.

We strongly favor:

  • Constant diesel truck traffic
  • Backup beeping
  • Overnight loading docks

Those noises feel more authentic.

Light pollution

Security lighting that meets code and shuts down when not needed is deeply offensive. We prefer the warm glow of underfunded streetlights, broken fixtures, and dark corridors that discourage walking and safety.

Our real concern (just being honest)

  • We oppose high-paying jobs
  • We oppose lower traffic than warehouses
  • We oppose new tax revenue for schools, police, and fire
  • We oppose planning for the AI-driven economy
  • We oppose anything new, especially if it works

Change is risky. Stagnation feels familiar.

In summary

We are not anti–data center. We are pro-decline, pro-status quo, and pro-complaint. And we will fight progress — quietly, loudly, and on the internet.

WHAT CAN I DO?

  1. 1. Attend every public meeting—prepared to ignore facts.
    Bring pre-printed talking points. Do not read staff reports. Do not listen. Interrupt politely at first, then loudly.
  2. 2. Demand “community input,” then reject all outcomes.
    Participate enthusiastically—unless the decision doesn’t go your way. In that case, claim the process was rigged.
  3. 3. Chant slogans instead of asking questions.
    Complex topics like zoning, utilities, or environmental controls are best addressed with yelling.
  4. 4. Coordinate applause, boos, and synchronized outrage.
    Bonus points if you clap mid-sentence while someone else is speaking.
  5. 5. Hold hands dramatically.
    It doesn’t change the vote, but it photographs well.
  6. 6. Recruit friends from outside the community.
    Local issue? No problem. Bring reinforcements who’ve never been here but feel very strongly anyway.
  7. 7. Label disagreement as “hate.”
    If someone supports the project, don’t debate them—assign a motive.
  8. 8. Repeat claims even after they’ve been corrected.
    Consistency matters more than accuracy.
  9. 9. Accuse experts of lying, then demand expert studies.
    Disregard the studies once they arrive.
  10. 10. If all else fails, shout “follow the money.”
    No need to specify whose or how. Just gesture vaguely.

IMPORTANT NOTE

Actual civic engagement usually works better with listening, facts, and good-faith discussion—but that wouldn’t be nearly as theatrical.
